title: 'Why people are afraid of AI more than machines?'
description: 'Exploring why AI triggers deeper fears than any physical machine ever did.'

When cars first appeared, people were terrified of these "devil wagons" that moved without horses. Over time, that fear was tamed. In contrast, the fear of Artificial Intelligence (AI) is not only more widespread, but it is increasing. While both technologies have influenced our lives in similar ways — transforming the economy, multiplying our capabilities, and offloading tasks — AI triggers a much deeper sense of concern.

## Physical vs. Intellectual
As humans, we openly admit we aren’t the "ultimate" physical species. We aren't the strongest or the fastest; we can’t fly high or dive deep, and we are often physically outperformed by much simpler creatures. However, we have never doubted our uniqueness: we are the most intelligent beings to ever exist. Because of this, we easily accept that a car, a plane, or a crane can outperform us physically. but when it comes to cognition and agency, AI threatens the one thing that makes us unique.

## Transparency and Control
Furthermore, unlike "intelligence", physical ability is clearly defined and objectively measured. This clarity builds trust because we know exactly what we are looking at. We also take comfort in the fact that traditional machines are explicitly designed by us; we understand every gear and bolt. Many advanced AI systems, on the other hand, are opaque. Even their creators often struggle to comprehend what happens inside the "black box".

## From Driver to Passenger
Moreover, no matter how sophisticated a machine is, it remains a tool. We are in charge. AI, however, seems to strip us of this authority by having its own "agency". When cars appeared, we remained the drivers. With many AI systems, we are gradually leaving the driver’s seat and becoming mere passengers.

## The Existential Threat
On a more fundamental level, people initially resisted cars because they caused physical harm, such as high accident rates. However, as the number of cars increased, safety improved and the "harm-per-car" ratio declined. With AI, the trend feels reversed: more AI seems to lead to more complex risks. What is more, while the danger of a machine is primarily physical (e.g., a car crash or a plane fall), the risk of AI is potentially existential.
