---
title: 'AI does not get bored'
description: 'Highlighting the gap between machine predictability and the human drive for meaning.'
pubDate: '03 Apr 2026'
---

"Good" is generally aligned with "predictable". To behave well is to act exactly as expected. A good answer to a math question is the correct one. There is a standard we try to meet or an objective goal to achieve. Similarly, an AI model is a predicting machine. A good prediction is simply an accurate one; the one that perfectly aligns with its training.

## You predicted it right... but...
Here is the twist: we sometimes get bored. And boredom becomes a trigger to explore uncertainty. Imagine waiting at the post office; nothing is happening and everything is going exactly as expected. With time, boredom builds up, and our brains try to fight it by making noise, shifting posture, or checking our phones for something unknown. We sometimes make decisions just because... "why not?". In fact, predicting correctly all the time is boring. We explore the uncertain; we cross oceans into the unknown, put on wings to fly like birds, or leave our comfortable homes to camp in a forest. Boredom itself becomes a hidden motivator, pushing us to look for the novel and the unusual.

Traditional AI training does not allow for this kind of exploration. Instead, the model is punished for stepping out of line, which often makes its behavior rigid. To overcome this, developers created "curiosity-driven" AI. These machines are given digital rewards for exploring highly uncertain situations. As the AI learns and the unknown becomes predictable, the reward drops. In this sense, an AI gets "bored" when it can perfectly predict what happens next. The reward system mimics our own hidden motivations... But, is "boredom" caused only by the predictable? And does an AI really feel "bored"?

## Unpredictable is not always meaningful
To an AI, a TV showing white noise is highly unpredictable. Its curiosity stays high, and it never gets "bored". For us, on the other hand, staring at static is incredibly dull. This implies that "unpredictable" does not always mean "meaningful". Boredom is more than just knowing what happens next. In fact, we look for something more: real value. We care about the "why", even if the reason is just "why not", rather than simply optimizing a mathematical reward function. For an AI, engineering "meaning" is not as easy as designing a system that looks for novelty. We can tweak a setting to make a chatbot more "creative," but the machine doesn't care if it's just spitting out gibberish.

One way to overcome the "noisy TV" problem is by teaching the AI to ignore pure randomness, like static or a coin flip[^1]. Another way is pushing the AI to only care about things it can actually influence[^2]. But... does this really close the meaning gap?

## Limited resources and opportunity cost
What makes us human is that value is tied to our internal goals, our identity, and our sense of self[^3]. We might be perfectly capable of focusing on a task, like counting grains of sand, but if that task means nothing to us, it quickly becomes boring[^4].

Our brains constantly monitor what we are doing and compare it to what we could be doing instead. Doing this requires knowing who we are, tracking our limited energy, and imagining "what if" scenarios. We live in a world of limited resources, so wasting time on boring activities is terribly inefficient. Even if exploring the unknown exposes us to risk, it isn't always an existential threat. The simple fact that our time and energy are limited pushes us forward. We don't just act to survive the moment; we act to save ourselves in the future. That requires long-term planning, not just reacting to instant rewards.

Knowing ourselves, watching our resources, imagining alternatives, and planning for the long-term are still largely missing from many AI systems. Until machines can do all of that, perhaps we can claim that we still have something special: we feel bored.
